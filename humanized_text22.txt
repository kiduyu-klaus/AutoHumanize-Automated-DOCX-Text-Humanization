Introduction
For instance, Rick Perlstein's Before the Storm: Barry Goldwater and the Unmaking of the American Consensus, Hill & Wang, 2001, is listed as a comparative resource regarding the trajectory of American political history, indicating the emergence of the modern conservative movement. This well thought out look at history shows how Barry Goldwater’s run for president in 1964 was not just a loss in politics, but a key point that changed what happened next in the U.S. It set the stage for big changes in government that led to Ronald Reagan and other later shifts. This, however, is more than ten years back, and the detail on which these seven hundred and sixty pages are so generously lavished is evidence that the author has not only learned the art of narration, but, what is even more difficult, the art of history, for he shows that what for so long has been trumpeted as the decade that changed the world was in fact that a liberal countercultural decade that the conservative insurgency was quietly laying the foundations of, which, some day, was to tilt the balance of history in its favour.

Author's Background and Approach
Author and historian Rick Perlstein offers a new perspective through his work as a writer because he can seamlessly combine the scholarly with the storytelling. His position is essentially revisionist, aiming to recover a narrative that was suppressed or displaced in most 1960s texts. Perlstein employs narrative history, characterized by compelling storytelling and character examination, while maintaining high standards for evidence and analytical rigor. His diction is poetic, cinematic, and makes politicians, for the reader, three dimensional figures, so that a large readership can attend the ideological wars of history without the loss of an ounce of brain. The author adopts a pro-conservative perspective, operating under the initial assumption that the significant electoral defeat experienced by Barry Goldwater in 1964 does not signify the end of conservatism but rather its commencement. This unconventional logic prompts Perlstein to approach the election outcome from a lateral perspective, analyzing it not as a voter but as an observer of the infrastructure, ideological trajectory, and populist appeal cultivated by the campaign. However, his rhetoric remains conciliatory and sympathetic towards the right wing protesters, though he is critically engaged, seeking to interpret the movement from within rather than as a conservative attack on liberalism.

Perlstein describes Before the Storm as a narrative set post-Eisenhower era, where moderate Republicans such as Nelson Rockefeller wielded influence within the party, and the prevailing liberal consensus appeared unassailable. The book opens in 1950s america, where most americans, democrats and republicans alike, enthusiastically embraced the new deal welfare state and cold war containment. This book shows the fights and mess between groups of republicans, the choices made by people who planned their moves, and the strong views that made some go on their own way. Perlstein underscores Goldwaters provocative declaration extremism in the name of liberty is a virtue as a fundamental characteristic of the movement, indicating a preference for ideological extremism over practical political strategy. The entire campaign for president was a detailed record, and Perlstein provides an analysis of how the candidate s errors influenced Goldwaters decisive defeat and which issues would have long-lasting relevance. They say that the landslide victory of lyndon johnson was not meant as a shot at liberalism, but only as a temporary interruption for a movement which was just about to change the whole character of american political life. Perlstein's primary thesis is that the 1964 campaign established the foundation for subsequent conservative successes, as it offered a structure for organization, enabled supporters to become active and engaged, improved messaging clarity and impact, and, most significantly, fueled the belief that ideological dedication could sustain political efforts despite election setbacks. In this book, Perlstein shows us many linked things: how a strong right-wing media grew, how rich people gave a lot of money to help, how church people came together, how worries in the middle class got used when things started to change, and how talk against smart people and big names spread. He contends that the goldwater campaign represents the initial phase of ideological divergence within u. s. politics and the breakdown of the postwar bipartisan consensus. People had grown tired of big, far-off government run by rules, and the fast changes in the world made them feel uneasy. Those who felt this way would be pulled to these simple plans.

Author utilizes contemporary sources, including newspapers, magazines, and televised coverage, to record the ambiance created by the campaign and depict the enthusiasm exhibited by the nation.
